A Decade of Change

The 1970s were defined by political scandal, social upheaval, environmental awakening, and cultural revolution. Here's how the decade unfolded.

1970
Social
Environment

Kent State & Earth Day

On May 4, 1970, National Guard troops opened fire on student protesters at Kent State University in Ohio, killing four students and wounding nine others. The shocking event galvanized the anti-Vietnam War movement. Just weeks earlier, on April 22, the very first Earth Day drew 20 million Americans into the streets, launching the modern environmental movement and leading to the creation of the EPA later that year.

1972
Politics
Science

Watergate Break-In & Apollo 17

On June 17, five men were caught breaking into the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate complex in Washington D.C. — a scandal that would bring down a presidency. Meanwhile, in December, astronauts Gene Cernan and Harrison Schmitt became the last humans to walk on the Moon during the Apollo 17 mission. Title IX was also signed into law, prohibiting sex discrimination in federally funded education programs and transforming women's sports forever.

1973
Law
Economy

Roe v. Wade & The Oil Crisis

The Supreme Court's landmark Roe v. Wade decision legalized abortion nationwide, transforming American politics and culture for generations. That same year, OPEC imposed an oil embargo against the US in response to American support for Israel during the Yom Kippur War. Gas prices quadrupled overnight, creating long lines at gas stations and triggering the worst economic recession since the Great Depression.

1974
Politics

Nixon Resigns

On August 9, 1974, Richard Nixon became the only U.S. President ever to resign from office, facing near-certain impeachment over his role in the Watergate cover-up. His memorable farewell address and helicopter departure from the White House lawn remain iconic television moments. Vice President Gerald Ford assumed the presidency and controversially pardoned Nixon one month later, declaring that "our long national nightmare is over."

1975
War

Vietnam War Ends

On April 30, 1975, the Fall of Saigon marked the end of the Vietnam War as North Vietnamese forces captured the South Vietnamese capital. Dramatic images of the final helicopter evacuations from the US Embassy roof became enduring symbols of American withdrawal. The war had claimed over 58,000 American lives and fundamentally shook public trust in the US government. Elsewhere, Muhammad Ali defeated Joe Frazier in the legendary "Thrilla in Manila."

1976
Culture

The Bicentennial

America celebrated 200 years of independence with enormous fanfare. Tall Ships sailed into New York Harbor, fireworks lit every city, and patriotic fervor swept the nation. Jimmy Carter was elected president over Gerald Ford. The Apple I computer was introduced by Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak. The country was in a mood of reflection — proud of its history, yet sobered by the turbulent years it had just survived.

1977
Entertainment

Star Wars & Saturday Night Fever

George Lucas's Star Wars premiered on May 25, 1977 and became an instant cultural phenomenon, changing Hollywood blockbusters forever. The same year, Saturday Night Fever — starring John Travolta — catapulted disco from underground clubs to mainstream America and made the Bee Gees household names. Elvis Presley died at 42 in August, sending the world into mourning. The first Apple II computers went on sale, and the Atari 2600 launched the home video game era.

1978–1979
Environment
Crisis

Love Canal, Three Mile Island & Iran

The Love Canal disaster in Niagara Falls, NY — where a neighborhood was built on a toxic chemical dump — revealed the devastating human cost of industrial pollution and galvanized the environmental movement. In March 1979, the Three Mile Island nuclear power plant in Pennsylvania suffered the worst nuclear accident in US history. And on November 4, Iranian militants seized the US Embassy in Tehran, taking 66 Americans hostage in a crisis that would define the end of the Carter presidency.

"Our long national nightmare is over. Our Constitution works; our great Republic is a government of laws and not of men." — President Gerald Ford, August 9, 1974
Style & Fashion

Dress to Impress

From the earthy hippie holdovers of the late 60s to the glittery excess of disco, 1970s fashion was bold, expressive, and unlike anything before or since.

EARLY 70s: EARTH & BOHEMIAN

The decade opened with a strong bohemian influence — a continuation of the late 60s counterculture. Earth tones dominated: burnt orange, avocado green, harvest gold, and chocolate brown. Women wore maxi skirts, peasant blouses, and crochet tops. Men embraced wide lapels, turtlenecks, and the iconic leisure suit. Both sexes wore their hair long and natural.

LATE 70s: DISCO & GLAM

By mid-decade, the disco explosion transformed fashion. Sequins, metallic fabrics, and stretch polyester replaced denim and cotton. Halter tops, jumpsuits, and wrap dresses (popularized by Diane Von Furstenberg in 1974) became ubiquitous. Platform shoes gave way to stilettos on the dance floor. Designer jeans — Jordache, Gloria Vanderbilt, Calvin Klein — emerged as status symbols by decade's end.

🛎️
Bell-Bottom Jeans
The defining trouser of the era — tight at the thigh and dramatically flared from the knee down. The wider the bell, the cooler the wearer.
👡
Platform Shoes
Towering soles up to 4 inches high, worn by men and women alike. Artists like KISS took it to theatrical extremes with platform boots.
🕺
Leisure Suit
The quintessential 70s menswear — a matching blazer and trousers in polyester, often in tan, powder blue, or cream, with wide lapels.
🌸
Wrap Dress
Diane Von Furstenberg's 1974 creation sold 5 million units in two years. Jersey fabric, vibrant prints — effortlessly chic and practical.
🧣
Macramé Everything
The knotting art form appeared on vests, bags, wall hangings, and plant holders. Handmade and earthy — a perfect expression of 70s DIY bohemia.
💍
Mood Ring
Invented in 1975, these liquid crystal rings supposedly changed color to reflect your emotional state. Blue meant calm; black meant stressed. Everyone had one.
✨
Halter Top & Hotpants
Disco-era staples. The halter bared the shoulders; hotpants were shorts so short they scandalized parents. Both were worn with platforms.
🪖
Afro & Natural Hair
The Afro became a powerful symbol of Black pride and identity, embodied by icons like Angela Davis, Pam Grier, and the Jackson 5.
😎
Tinted Aviators
The oversized aviator frame — worn with a tint in amber, blue, or green — became as synonymous with the 70s as any garment.
"Fashion is not something that exists in dresses only. Fashion is in the sky, in the street; fashion has to do with ideas, the way we live, what is happening." — Coco Chanel (often quoted in 70s fashion magazines)
Music

The Soundtrack of a Decade

The 70s produced some of the greatest music in American history — spanning disco, classic rock, funk, soul, singer-songwriter folk, and the birth of punk. Click any song to learn more.

GENRE EXPLOSION

No decade produced more diverse musical genres simultaneously. Classic rock reached its artistic peak with Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd, and The Eagles. Funk and soul flourished with Stevie Wonder, Earth Wind & Fire, and Marvin Gaye. Then disco erupted in 1977, dividing America into two camps — those who loved it and those who burned their records.

THE DISCO REVOLUTION

Born in underground Black and LGBTQ+ clubs, disco went mainstream with Saturday Night Fever (1977). Studio 54 in New York became the world's most exclusive club. By 1979, "Disco Sucks" was a rallying cry — culminating in Disco Demolition Night at Comiskey Park where 50,000 fans destroyed disco records on the field.

🎸
Classic Rock's Golden Era
Led Zeppelin, Pink Floyd (Dark Side of the Moon — the best-selling album of all time — arrived in 1973), Fleetwood Mac (Rumours, 1977), and The Eagles defined stadium rock.
🎹
Singer-Songwriters
Carole King's Tapestry (1971) spent 15 weeks at #1. James Taylor, Carly Simon, and Jackson Browne brought introspective folk into the mainstream.
🎤
The Birth of Hip-Hop
On August 11, 1973, DJ Kool Herc threw a back-to-school party in the Bronx and invented hip-hop by isolating the percussion breaks in funk records. A cultural revolution began.
⚡
Punk Arrives
By 1976–77, punk exploded as a reaction to disco excess. The Ramones, The Clash, and Patti Smith brought raw energy back to rock. The Sex Pistols' American tour (1978) ended in chaos.
Sports

Champions & Legends

The 1970s produced some of the greatest athletes and moments in American sports history — from Ali's legendary fights to the Steelers dynasty, to a horse that captured the nation.

🥊
Muhammad Ali
Ali dominated boxing throughout the decade. The "Rumble in the Jungle" (1974) against George Foreman in Zaire — where Ali used his "rope-a-dope" strategy — is considered the greatest sporting event of the 20th century. The "Thrilla in Manila" against Joe Frazier (1975) was so brutal both fighters nearly died in the ring.
3× World Heavyweight Champion
🏈
Pittsburgh Steelers Dynasty
The Steelers became the team of the decade, winning four Super Bowls in six years (1974–79). Led by Terry Bradshaw, Franco Harris, Lynn Swann, and the "Steel Curtain" defense, they were arguably the greatest dynasty in NFL history. The "Immaculate Reception" (1972) may be the most famous play in football history.
Super Bowls IX, X, XIII, XIV
🏇
Secretariat
In 1973, Secretariat became the first horse in 25 years to win the Triple Crown. His Belmont Stakes performance — winning by 31 lengths in a world-record time that still stands — is considered the greatest performance by any thoroughbred in history. His time of 2:24 has never been broken.
Triple Crown 1973 · Belmont Record: 2:24
🎾
Battle of the Sexes
On September 20, 1973, Billie Jean King defeated Bobby Riggs in straight sets before 30,000 fans at the Houston Astrodome and 90 million TV viewers worldwide. Riggs had boasted no woman could beat him. King's victory became one of the defining moments of the women's rights movement.
King def. Riggs: 6–4, 6–3, 6–3
🏀
The ABA & NBA Merger
The rival American Basketball Association, known for its red-white-blue ball, flashy play, and stars like Julius "Dr. J" Erving, merged with the NBA in 1976. Four ABA teams joined the NBA, and the merger transformed professional basketball. The Kareem Abdul-Jabbar–led Milwaukee Bucks and LA Lakers dominated early in the decade.
ABA–NBA Merger: 1976
⚾
The Big Red Machine
The Cincinnati Reds of 1975–76 — featuring Pete Rose, Johnny Bench, Joe Morgan, and Ken Griffey Sr. — are considered one of the greatest baseball teams ever assembled. Their 1975 World Series against the Red Sox, in which Carlton Fisk's iconic home run (Game 6) stands as one of the most dramatic moments in baseball history.
World Series Champs: 1975, 1976
🏒
Bobby Orr & the Bruins
Bobby Orr redefined what a defenseman could be, winning the Norris Trophy eight consecutive times. His flying goal that won the 1970 Stanley Cup — captured in the iconic photograph where he soars through the air — became one of sport's most reproduced images. He scored 102 points in 1970–71 as a defenseman, a record that still stands.
8× Norris Trophy · 2× Stanley Cup
🏃
Running Boom
The 1970s saw a massive jogging and running craze sweep America. Frank Shorter won marathon gold at the 1972 Munich Olympics, inspiring millions. Bill Rodgers won four Boston and four NYC Marathons. Nike, founded in 1971, rode the wave to become a global brand. By 1978, an estimated 25 million Americans were running regularly.
Nike Founded: 1971
Pop Culture & Fads

Can You Dig It?

The 70s gave America some of its most wonderfully bizarre fads and crazes — from rocks sold as pets to trucks controlled by CB radio chatter.

🐾
Pet Rocks
In 1975, Gary Dahl sold smooth stones from Mexico's Rosarito Beach as "pets" — packaged in a cardboard box with straw bedding and a humorous care manual. Each sold for $3.95. Dahl sold 1.5 million in six months, becoming a millionaire. The manual explained how to teach your rock to "sit" and "stay." Pet Rocks are the ultimate symbol of 70s absurdist consumer culture.
💿
Disco & Studio 54
The disco era transformed nightlife. Studio 54 in NYC became the world's most exclusive club — celebrities, socialites, and everyday people all fought for entry. The velvet rope changed nightlife forever.
📻
CB Radios
Citizen Band radios went from truck driver tools to mainstream craze after the 1975 hit "Convoy" by C.W. McCall. "10–4, Good Buddy" entered everyday speech. By 1977, 12 million CB licenses had been issued.
🌋
Lava Lamps
Originally invented in 1963, lava lamps hit peak popularity in the early 70s as symbols of the psychedelic era. Every dorm room and bachelor pad had one. They're still sold today — a testament to groovy design.
🎳
Roller Disco
As disco and roller skating collided, roller rinks became the social hubs of suburban America. Roller disco was the perfect synthesis of the decade's two biggest crazes. Films like Roller Boogie (1979) captured the moment.
🧩
Rubik's Cube
Invented by Ernő Rubik in 1974 (sold in Hungary from 1977), the Cube reached the US market by 1980 — but it became a 70s icon in Europe and was already driving people mad by decade's end.
🏃
Streaking
Running naked in public became a brief but memorable craze in 1974. A streaker ran across the Academy Awards stage. Colleges reported thousands of incidents. Ray Stevens' novelty song "The Streak" hit #1.
🎮
Pong & Arcade Games
Atari released Pong in 1972 — the first commercially successful video game. By the late 70s, Space Invaders (1978) caused a coin shortage in Japan and Pac-Man was on its way. The video game age had arrived.
🌿
Macramé, Tie-Dye & the DIY Aesthetic
The 70s were the golden age of craft culture. Macramé (decorative knotting) appeared on walls, plant holders, clothing, and bags. Tie-dye — a 60s holdover — remained hugely popular. Decoupage, ceramics, and weaving classes filled community centers. The era's earth-conscious, anti-consumerist spirit expressed itself through making things by hand. The 1970 debut of Mother Earth News magazine gave the movement its bible.
